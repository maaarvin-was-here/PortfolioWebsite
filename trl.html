Note: A lot of what I did for this project is confidential and the proprietary knowledge of the lab, and hence not shared publicly here

I spent the summer as a robotics research intern at the University of Washington's Transformative Robotics Laboratory designing, building, and testing prototypes for a novel robotic joint mechanism. I created a number of CAD designs (drawings and models) with design for manufacturability in mind using SolidWorks. I also utilized rapid prototyping methods to quickly make and test different iterations of various components against a set of test criteria.

I especially enjoyed working with the lab's Carbon M1 3D Digital Light Processing (DLP) printer, for which I learned printing, handling, and curing methods for a variety of different resin materials including UMA90 and RPU70. Thanks to a combination of accurate CAD modeling and the Carbon's extreme precision, the parts came out of the printer fairly well. However, I would always perform more precision machining using machine shop tools and small hand tools in order to account for small variances and get the part to be sized just right, contributing to more effective and valid testing.

I also wrote scripts in Python to actuate the motion of the joint using inverse kinematics equations, which control one of three brushless DC motors acting in a dimension, which allows the mechanism's end effector to be moved to any point on a 2D plane.
